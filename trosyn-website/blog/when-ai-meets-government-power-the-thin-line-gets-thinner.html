When AI Meets Government Power, the "Thin Line" Gets Thinner

Mar 25, 2026 · 4 min read · Ivan Santongo

The situation between Anthropic and the U.S. government shows just how thin that line is becoming between what governments want from AI and what companies believe is responsible to build.

This isn’t a simple good vs. bad story.

It’s a story about power, safety, values, and long-term trust. And it’s a preview of a future where AI isn’t just a product category, it’s a strategic asset that governments will try to steer the same way they steer oil, telecoms, and defence supply chains.

Anthropic and the U.S. Government

The conflict, from public reporting, comes down to this:

The U.S. defence establishment wants stronger access and fewer restrictions. It believes AI is now central to national security, and it wants to use it that way.

Anthropic tried to hold the line. Particularly against anything resembling autonomous weapons support or broad domestic surveillance, arguing the technology — and the safeguards around it aren’t ready for that kind of use.

That disagreement didn’t stay quiet.

On February 27, 2026, President Trump directed federal agencies to phase out Anthropic’s technology. The Pentagon labelled Anthropic a “supply-chain risk.” Anthropic said it would challenge the designation in court.

Whatever your position on it, this is a loud signal. Governments are no longer just talking about AI policy. They’re enforcing it.

And here’s the part people keep missing: the government’s response wasn’t just “we disagree.” It was “we will cut you out of the system.”

That’s what it looks like when AI becomes strategic.

What This Reveals Beyond the Headlines

This moment exposes three uncomfortable truths.

Governments don’t like dependency. When agencies rely on a model in critical workflows, they want control over the terms, the availability, and the restrictions. Especially in defence contexts.

Company values get stress-tested at scale. It’s easy to publish safety principles on a website. It’s harder when a major government customer wants exceptions, speed, and capability — now.

Trust becomes political. The moment a model becomes part of the national infrastructure, every decision around it gets filtered through power. Ethics becomes politics. Politics becomes procurement.

This isn’t the end of responsible AI.

But it is the end of believing that responsibility lives only inside companies.

The Same Tension Closer to Home: Uganda’s Direction

In Uganda, we’re watching the early shape of the same debate — how to encourage innovation while protecting rights and avoiding harm.

Uganda is moving toward more formal AI governance: risk-based rules, transparency requirements, oversight, and stronger guardrails in sensitive sectors.

On paper, that’s a good sign. It shows seriousness.

But from a builder’s perspective, it can feel like walking a tightrope.

Too relaxed, and people get harmed quietly, especially in health, finance, and security. Too strict, and innovation gets choked before it has room to grow.

The tricky part is how regulation tends to build: layer by layer. Registration. Documentation. Impact assessments. Monitoring. Approvals. Reporting.

Each requirement can make sense on its own. Together, they can become a wall, especially for small teams working without legal departments or compliance budgets.

Walking the Thin Line

The Anthropic case and Uganda’s AI policy direction are two sides of the same coin.

Both show that governments are moving from watching AI to actively steering it. That companies are being forced to decide what they will not compromise on, even when it costs contracts. The line between public interest and political interest can blur fast, especially once “security” becomes the trump card in every argument.

I’m not saying the answer is fear or cynicism.

But I do think we should be honest: this is a delicate phase.

We need governments that can listen and iterate, not just punish and restrict. We need companies that engage openly, not just lobby quietly behind closed doors. And we need governance frameworks that are rights-based, transparent, and shaped with public input because AI policy can’t be legitimate if it’s written only by insiders.

For those of us actually building things, the challenge is simple to say and hard to live:

Keep creating. But stay awake to the fact that law, policy, and ethics are now part of the design space, not something you deal with later.
