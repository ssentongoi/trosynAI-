Does AI Still Belong to Silicon Valley?

Apr 29, 2026 · 5 min read · Ivan Santongo

For years, technology had an address. Silicon Valley.

If you wanted to build something big, you heard the same story on repeat: go where the talent is, raise money, hire fast, burn cash, and hope you become the next "something."

It was not just a myth. It was a system and for a long time, it worked.

But in 2026, something is shifting. And for the first time, the shift feels real.

AI is not just changing what software can do. It is changing how companies are built.

The Old Game: Big Teams, Big Budgets, Big Gravity

The assumption used to be simple: if you want to compete, you need scale.

That meant hiring 20, 30, 50 developers. Building departments early. Stacking headcount because headcount meant speed. And often, moving closer to where the talent and capital already lived.

Silicon Valley became powerful because it had gravity. The best people went there. The best deals happened there. The best stories came from there.

Everyone else watched from a distance.

The New Reality: More Output, Fewer People

AI breaks that logic.

A small team can now build like a big team, not because AI replaces people, but because it amplifies them.

A team of one to five strong builders can now write, review, and ship code faster. Plan features faster. Test ideas in days instead of months. Handle work that used to require a chain of specialists.

You still need smart humans. That has not changed.

What has changed is the ratio. Output per person is rising, and when that happens, the entire startup map starts to redraw itself.

Bootstrapping Feels Real Again

This is the part that matters most for emerging markets, and honestly, for everyone.

In the past, bootstrapping was a slower road. You needed a team, a runway, infrastructure, and time. The barriers were structural.

Now, you can build a serious product with fewer people, less capital, and a lot more speed.

We are already seeing what that looks like.

One of the clearest examples is Base44, an AI app-building startup created by Israeli founder Maor Shlomo. Bootstrapped. Sold to Wix for around $80 million. Built in roughly six months.

That story would have sounded impossible a few years ago. Now it sounds like a preview.

Not because everyone will exit for $80M, they will not. But because it proves something that matters: small teams can now build products that travel far, fast, without being located inside the old centres of power.

Remote Work + AI = The Valley Is Still Great, Just Less Necessary

Silicon Valley is not going away. The talent, the networks, the capital are all still there.

But the dream has shifted.

Before: "If I get to Silicon Valley, I can finally build something great."

Now: "I can build something great from anywhere, and Silicon Valley is just one option."

Remote work started that shift. AI is accelerating it.

When small teams can move faster and build more, the advantage of being in one physical location shrinks. Not to zero. But enough to matter.

The Real Equaliser: Distributed Capability

This is the idea I keep coming back to.

AI is a distributed capability.

It does not erase Silicon Valley's influence, but it spreads opportunity wider. It rebalances the market.

You do not need a massive team to start. You do not need a massive budget to test. You do not need a massive office to ship.

What you need is clarity, taste, speed, discipline, and the ability to work with AI like a partner, not a shortcut.

For the first time in a long time, we are watching a genuine equaliser enter the system. And I do not think we have fully understood how big that shift will become.

Not yet.

Closing Thought

Silicon Valley will always matter.

But in 2026, it matters differently.

It used to be a gate.

Now it is more like a station: somewhere you can pass through, learn from, and connect with, but not somewhere you have to be.

You do not have to live there to build something that reaches the world.

And that is new.
